02 — Compression

730 hours a year.

The ILO/ISO curve applied to Nouakchott's temperatures gives a precise result. Not a range. A number. And that number has a price.

Applied hour by hour to Nouakchott's temperature data, the ILO/ISO curve produces an annual average of 730 hours of lost work capacity per outdoor worker. That is the equivalent of 91 working days — nearly four months.

To convert hours to money, we use the Mauritanian SMIG as a lower bound — 3,000 MRU/month (ILO ILOSTAT, 2022). This is deliberately conservative: informal workers in Nouakchott often earn more. The real loss is probably higher.

At the central scenario (1.4× SMIG), a worker loses 14,742 MRU per year — 41% of their annual minimum wage, dissolved into heat before they could earn it.

October: 117 hours lost. Nearly three weeks of work.

July, against all expectation, is one of the most moderate months — only 38.4 hours lost. The harmattan reduces humidity and moderates effective heat. This result is not intuitive. That is why we measured.

All values are averages over 2019–2025. Stability year to year strengthens confidence in the number.

All three scenarios use the same hours lost (730h). Only the hourly wage varies. The conservative scenario anchors to the legal SMIG. The central applies a 1.4× multiplier — consistent with ILO and World Bank studies on informal wages in urban West Africa. The upper represents skilled tradespeople and small entrepreneurs.

120,000 exposed workers — our central estimate for Nouakchott, based on INS data on the informal sector (91.1% of non-agricultural private employment) and the share of outdoor moderate-intensity work (~40%).
