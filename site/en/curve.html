The work capacity curve is not something we invented. It comes from the ILO report Working on a Warmer Planet (2019) and ISO 7243:2017 — the international occupational heat stress standards used by governments, employers, and researchers worldwide.

We apply it at moderate intensity — 200 to 300 watts of metabolic rate. That is the level representative of a market vendor standing at a stall, a bricklayer carrying blocks, or a docker unloading crates. Not an air-conditioned office, not extreme physical labour. The middle of Nouakchott.

Below 24°C, capacity is 100%. Heat costs nothing. Above 33°C, the curve drops quickly. At 34°C, a worker can only produce half of what they would in normal conditions. At 40°C, they are running at 20%.

The hardest month is not July. It is October.

The harmattan — the dry Saharan wind — moderates temperatures in July. October has no such relief. Humid heat settles in, average temperatures reach 32.5°C, and compression climbs to 37.8%. Nearly twice January.

This result contradicts intuition. Most people assume July is the worst month. The data says October. That is why we measured.

Three professions, one curve

Behavioural compression

Market vendors

Marché Capitale, Marché 5ème. Standing all day. No AC. Heat sets the rhythm.

Physical compression

Masons and day labourers

Construction, handling, public works. Sustained physical effort under direct sun. The curve applies literally.

Financial compression

Avenue Nasser shops

Boutiques, workshops, small offices. Those who can afford AC stay open. The rest slow down or close.
