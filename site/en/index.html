01 — The Curve

At 34°C, a worker runs at 50%.

The ILO/ISO occupational heat stress standard.

This curve comes from the ILO and ISO 7243 — international occupational heat stress standards. We didn't build it. We applied it to six years of hourly temperature data for Nouakchott.

Work capacity (%) by temperature — ILO (2019) + ISO 7243:2017, moderate intensity. Market vendors, construction workers, port workers.

The hardest month is not July. It is October — 37.8% compression.

The harmattan moderates July. October has no such excuse.

02 — Compression Rate

730 hours per year. Per worker.

Applied to Nouakchott's temperatures, 2019–2025.

Applied to Nouakchott's temperatures, the ILO/ISO curve yields 730 hours lost per year for an outdoor worker at moderate intensity. At Mauritania's minimum wage (3,000 MRU/month, ILO 2022), that is 14,742 MRU lost at the central estimate — 41% of the annual minimum wage. This is a lower bound.

Working hours lost to heat by month — 2019–2025 average.

  • Hours lost / year 730h
  • Cost per worker (central estimate) 14,742 MRU
  • As % of annual minimum wage 41%
  • City-wide income loss (central) 1,769M MRU ≈ $49M
  • Worst month October — 117h

03 — The AC Premium

Here, AC is not comfort.

It is the price of staying open.

In Nouakchott, air conditioning separates those who keep working from those who stop. It is not a luxury — it is an economic survival condition in a 45°C city.

From the JICA 2018 Urban Master Plan (Table I-14), we estimate 94,296 commercial spaces across the city. One in three has AC. At SOMELEC's commercial rate of 5.903 MRU/kWh:

  • Conservative scenario — 1 in 5 521M MRU ≈ $14.5M
  • Central scenario — 1 in 3 869M MRU ≈ $24M
  • Upper scenario — 1 in 2 1,304M MRU ≈ $36M

869M MRU spent just to stay open. Not to profit — to survive the summer.
